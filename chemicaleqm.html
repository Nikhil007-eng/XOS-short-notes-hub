1. Introduction to Chemical Equilibrium

Reversible vs Irreversible Reactions

Irreversible Reaction Reversible Reaction
Proceeds in forward direction only Proceeds in both directions
Does not attain equilibrium Attains equilibrium state
Represented by single arrow (→) Represented by double arrow (↔)
Examples:
• Precipitation reactions
• Neutralisation reactions
• Decomposition of solids in open vessel		 Examples:
• Homogeneous reactions
• Heterogeneous reactions
Key Concept: At equilibrium, the rate of forward reaction equals the rate of backward reaction.

2. Equilibrium Constant

Law of Mass Action

For a general reversible reaction:

aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Equilibrium Constant in Terms of Concentration (Kc)

Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b

Equilibrium Constant in Terms of Partial Pressure (Kp)

Kp = (PC)c(PD)d / (PA)a(PB)b

Relationship Between Kp and Kc

Kp = Kc(RT)Δng

where Δng = (moles of gaseous products) - (moles of gaseous reactants)

Example:

For the reaction: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)

Δng = 2 - (1 + 3) = -2

Kp = Kc(RT)-2

3. Reaction Quotient (Q)

For reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Qc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b (at any time)

Comparison with Equilibrium Constant:

  • If Q < K → Reaction proceeds in forward direction
  • If Q > K → Reaction proceeds in backward direction
  • If Q = K → System is at equilibrium

4. Factors Affecting Equilibrium Constant

1. Stoichiometry of Reaction

If reaction is multiplied by n, then Knew = (K)n

2. Reversing the Reaction

If A ⇌ B has K1, then B ⇌ A has K2 = 1/K1

3. Temperature Dependence (Van't Hoff Equation)

ln(K) = -ΔH°/R(1/T) + ΔS°/R

log(K₂/K₁) = ΔH°/(2.303R) × (1/T₁ - 1/T₂)

Effect of Temperature:

  • Endothermic reactions: K increases with increase in temperature
  • Exothermic reactions: K decreases with increase in temperature

5. Degree of Dissociation (α)

α = (moles dissociated at equilibrium) / (initial moles)

Relation with Equilibrium Constant

For reaction: A(g) ⇌ nB(g)

Kp = (n²α²P) / (1 - α²)

For reaction: A(g) ⇌ B(g) + C(g)

Kp = α²P / (1 - α²)

For reaction: A(g) ⇌ B(g) + ½C(g)

Kp = α³√P / [2(1-α)√(2+α)]

6. Vapor Density Method

For reaction: A(g) ⇌ nB(g)

α = (D - d) / [(n - 1)d]

where:

  • D = Theoretical vapor density (before dissociation)
  • d = Observed vapor density (after dissociation)
  • n = Number of moles of gaseous products

Example Calculation:

For PCl₅(g) ⇌ PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g), n = 2

α = (D - d) / d

7. Heterogeneous Equilibrium

All species are not in the same phase.

Important Examples:

  • CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
  • NH₂COONH₄(s) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) + CO₂(g)
  • C(s) + CO₂(g) ⇌ 2CO(g)

Important Note:

For heterogeneous equilibria, concentrations of pure solids and liquids are constant and not included in equilibrium expression.

8. Le Chatelier's Principle

"If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change of pressure, temperature or concentration, the equilibrium shifts to counteract the effect of that change."

Effects of Various Factors:

Change Applied Direction of Shift
Increase in reactant concentration Forward direction
Increase in product concentration Backward direction
Increase in pressure Towards side with fewer moles of gas
Decrease in pressure Towards side with more moles of gas
Increase in temperature Towards endothermic direction
Decrease in temperature Towards exothermic direction
Addition of inert gas at constant volume No effect
Addition of inert gas at constant pressure Towards side with more moles of gas
Addition of catalyst No effect on equilibrium position

9. Thermodynamics of Equilibrium

Relationship with Gibbs Free Energy

ΔG° = -RT ln K

ΔG° = ΔH° - TΔS°

Key Relationships:

  • If ΔG° < 0, then K > 1 (Products favored)
  • If ΔG° > 0, then K < 1 (Reactants favored)
  • If ΔG° = 0, then K = 1 (Equal amounts)

10. Multiple Equilibria

If: A ⇌ B (K₁) and B ⇌ C (K₂)

Then: A ⇌ C (K₃ = K₁ × K₂)

Example:

For stepwise formation of [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺:

Koverall = K₁ × K₂ × K₃ × K₄
